Two layers, one engine

Built to grow one sensor at a time

Gargoyle's detection engine is modular by design — each signal source is its own module that plugs into a shared confidence system.

Shipping now

Passive detection

Scans quietly for known public-safety equipment signatures over Bluetooth Low Energy. Repeated sightings are confirmed against a device-identity check before anything is surfaced, and a cooldown policy keeps alerts meaningful instead of noisy.

Planned

Crowdsourced reports

A second, opt-in layer where the community can share what they're seeing in real time — the same spirit as crowdsourced traffic alerts, purpose-built for situational awareness.

Under the hood

What's actually running on your phone

Every stage happens on-device. Nothing here talks to a server, because there isn't one.

SIGNAL SOURCE

A modular scanner interface. BLE ships first; Wi-Fi, cellular, and peripheral sensors register into the same engine as they're built, without touching what's already working.

CONFIDENCE ENGINE

Fuses whatever signals are active into a single low / medium / high read, using a rolling window so a single stray reading can't trigger a false alarm.

DEVICE MATCH CONFIRMER

Requires repeated sightings of the same device — not just the same equipment vendor — before confidence is allowed to climb.

ALERT POLICY

A cooldown layer that stands between detection and notification, so you hear from Gargoyle when it matters, not on every scan cycle.

LOCAL EVENT LOG

Everything Gargoyle sees stays on your device. No backend, no account, no network call — by design, not by omission.

No backend, on purpose

Privacy isn't a setting here

Gargoyle was designed so there's nothing to subpoena, because there's nothing collected.

Nothing leaves the device

No network calls in the current build. Detection, confirmation, and logging all happen locally.

No accounts, no tracking

There's nothing to sign into and nothing that identifies you — the app doesn't know who you are.

Open source, start to finish

The full Android Studio project is public — signature files, engine logic, and tests included.
